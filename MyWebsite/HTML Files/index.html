My Objective

The notion of hard work is often ill-conceived. Many times, we fail to recognize that hard work is not solely determined by effort, but how we use that effort to achieve what matters. In order to accomplish something meaningful, we have to consider which goals are important. In my case, I started to develop my understanding of hard work from the beginning of my fifth-grade essay writing course. As I walked into the classroom for the first time, the most memorable thing that I noticed was a large wall packed with shelves of seemingly random items. The teacher’s wall had many posters and pictures of things that made a statement about her identity, including a framed family portrait, a beaming student review, and the intimidating Wisconsin Badgers mascot. However, the item that caught my attention the most was a huge eraser sitting on her top shelf. On it were inscribed the words, “For BIG Mistakes”. Although I had never been particularly keen on using erasers because they signified redoing what was good enough, I started to ask myself what was good enough. “Use it wisely,” she said, after seeing me staring at it. Cherishing the gift of revision, I examined aspects of my life very carefully to remove the mistakes I found.
By the end of the year, the eraser was now a pile of eraser shavings soaked in what used to be writing. Throughout the month prior, our class had received and workshopped a major project involving an essay presented on a poster. After receiving a 95 on the first draft, I spent several hours late at night revising for those last few points. I had sacrificed my peace of mind and ability to relax for that last month just to marginally increase the quality of an old project and pass a mental boundary. But why did I do it? At the time, I believed that the core principle of hard work was to refuse to accept any standard short of perfection, as is human nature. However, I was forced to question this definition of hard work and whether it was worth the toil in later years. There were times when I found I had fewer hours in a day than it would take to complete every task flawlessly, and I soon realized that I would have to consider how much of what I accomplished mattered to me. The only way humanity has achieved anything meaningful is through the hard work and ingenuity of individuals. To answer the question, I considered the kind of impact I wanted to have on humankind. My vision for the future is one where humanity is more intelligent and efficient than currently possible with the help of sentient AI supplements. "Deep learning", a subset of machine learning, is rapidly improving in mimicking the human decision-making process, so investigating deep learning will further research in developing sentient AI. I believe that this is a goal worth fighting for, and it is the motivation for my hard work.
Throughout my time in high school, I strived to develop my understanding of computer science and machine learning to alter the state of technology in the future. To get practice in theoretical research, I worked as an intern at the Fermilab particle physics lab. My job was to generate graphs and displays of particle interaction data using C++ code and to present findings in the paper. This was my first experience conducting novel research, and I learned a lot about the subject. Although my work was not perfect, it made me realize that I did contribute to the field in some way. In the grand scheme of things, hard work is not defined by how close to perfect one’s work is but how by much one contributes to what matters to them.
