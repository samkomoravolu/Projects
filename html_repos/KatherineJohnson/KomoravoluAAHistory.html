Katherine Johnson

Who is Katherine Johnson?

by: Sameer Komoravolu

Intro
Katherine Johnson was a famous mathematician and physicist who worked at NASA. During her career, she helped pioneer the usage of computers to perform difficult calculations. Johnson calculated launch windows, trajectories and emergency return paths for Project Mercury. She was one of the first African-American women to work at NASA.


Education
Johnson was born on August 26, 1918 in West Virginia with three siblings. Joylette Coleman was her mother, and worked as a teacher. Her father was Joshua Coleman, who was a lumberman, handyman, and a farmer. From a young age, she had great mathematical capabilities. Graduating from the West Virginia institute at age 14, She attended West Virginia State. At this college, she competed every math course offered. At 18, she graduated summa cum laude.


Career
After graduating from college, Johnson became a teacher in 1937 in Marion, Virginia. In 1939, she left her job to enter a graduate math program. One year later, she quit to focus on her family. In 1953, the National Advisory Comittee for Aeronautics (NACA) offered he a job in the Guidance and Navigation Department. Initially, she worked in a group of women performing complex calculations. Until 1958, she carried out calculations reguarding gust alleviation for aircraft. On October 1st, 1958, NACA turned over operations to NASA, and Johnson shifted to an aerospace technologist, working in the Spacecraft Controls Branch. Johnson calculated the tracectory for the 1961 flight of Alan Shepard, the first American in space. Later in her career, she had worked with digital computers, and had helped gain confidence in computers. In 1986, Johnson had retired, having worked at NACA and NASA for 33 years.


Conclusion
Overall, Katherine Johnson has revolutionized the field of mathematics. She was an African-American woman in the field of math, which is difficult to accomplish. At NASA, she was able to perform difficult calculations with great accuracy, and introduced the use of computers to perform such tasks. Her work won many awards such as the Honorary Doctor of Science in 2006 and the Congressional Gold Medal on November 8th, 2019.
